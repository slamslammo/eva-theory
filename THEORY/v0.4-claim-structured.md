## Abstract

Most agent architectures are organized around **task completion**. Persistence, continuity, and self-integrity are either assumed or treated as auxiliary concerns. EVA begins from a different premise: for a certain class of agents, **continuous existence is the first-order constraint**, and other capabilities must be understood relative to that constraint.

This paper makes two distinct claims.

**Claim A (paradigm claim):** There exists a meaningful class of agents for which the task-centered framing is structurally insufficient. For such agents, the correct starting point is not "what task should the agent complete?" but "what must the agent maintain in order to persist as the same agent over time?"

**Claim B (structural claim):** Under three operating conditions—survival continuity, environmental non-stationarity, and finite encoding capacity—a layered architecture consisting of homeostatic sensing (L1), drive structure (L2), and adaptive deliberation (L3) is a structurally coherent response. Two further layers, self-model (L4) and social cognition (L5), are proposed as downstream extensions under additional pressures.

The contribution of this work is not a benchmark-oriented agent design. It is a new organizing framework for agents centered on continuity of existence. Within that framework, we make four engineering contributions: (1) **drives as continuous contextual state rather than instruction**, (2) **anchors as generative structural constraints rather than post-hoc rules**, (3) **explicit drive injection rather than opaque emergent drives**, and (4) **a basal ganglia analog as an independent peer circuit rather than a reasoning sub-module**.

We are explicit about scope and epistemic status. We do not claim uniqueness, universality, or finality. We claim that for the target problem class, EVA provides a principled architecture whose internal logic is stronger than current task-centered alternatives.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 The hidden assumption in current agent design

The dominant agent architectures—ReAct, AutoGPT, BabyAGI, LangGraph, and their descendants—share an assumption so pervasive it often disappears from view: **the agent exists in order to complete tasks**.

From this starting point, memory serves recall, reasoning serves planning, tools serve execution, and the lifecycle of the agent is bounded by the task boundary. This framing naturally produces a family of systems with recognizable properties:

- Session-bounded identity
- No intrinsic motivational substrate
- No stake in continuity
- Drift resistance treated as prompt engineering or external guardrailing
- No architectural distinction between "continue existing" and "do useful work"

These are not implementation bugs. They are structural consequences of a task-centered starting point.

### 1.2 A different starting point

EVA begins from a different question:

> **What would an agent need if its primary constraint were to continue existing as itself over time?**

This shift is not rhetorical. It changes the architecture.

| Task-centered agent | Existence-centered agent |
|---|---|
| Primary question: what should I do? | Primary question: what must I maintain to persist? |
| Task boundary defines lifecycle | No terminal state internal to the architecture |
| Memory serves recall | Memory serves threat recognition, skill formation, and continuity |
| Motivation is externally supplied | Motivation is architecturally internal |
| Guardrails are external checks | Anchors are structural constraints |
| Skills are installed | Skills crystallize from repeated successful action |

### 1.3 Two claims, not one

A recurring failure mode in theorizing about architectures is to mix two kinds of claim:

1. a **paradigm claim** about what problem we are solving
2. a **structural claim** about what architecture fits that problem

EVA makes both, but they should not be conflated.

- **Claim A** says the dominant problem framing is incomplete for a certain class of agents.
- **Claim B** says that, under specified conditions, a layered architecture is a coherent response.

Keeping these claims separate is essential. Claim A is broader and more philosophical; Claim B is narrower and more defensible.

### 1.4 Why biology appears here, and why this is not biomimicry

We use biological evolution as a reference system because it is the richest known source of systems that persist under pressure. Evolution does not tell us what we must build. It tells us that certain structural solutions have repeatedly emerged when persistence, competition, and adaptation mattered.

We do **not** claim:
- that biological structures are optimal for digital agents,
- that EVA should imitate the brain in detail,
- or that evolutionary history directly determines engineering design.

We do claim:
- that evolutionary pressures provide a useful filter for deciding which architectural elements are worth taking seriously,
- and that biological architectures serve as **existence proofs** that certain decompositions are workable.

## 2. Scope: When EVA Applies

EVA does not claim universal applicability. It applies to a specific class of systems.

### 2.1 Operating conditions

We assume three conditions.

**C1. Survival continuity requirement.**  
The value of the system depends on its continuous existence over time. Failure at an intermediate point is not an acceptable reset; continuity matters.

**C2. Environmental non-stationarity.**  
The system operates in an environment whose statistical structure changes over time. Past regularities are useful but not sufficient.

**C3. Finite encoding capacity.**  
No finite design-time specification can encode all relevant future states and responses.

### 2.2 Out of scope

EVA is not intended for:

- one-shot task agents,
- fully stationary environments,
- systems where continuity is irrelevant,
- or benchmark settings where repeated reset is costless.

For such systems, EVA may be unnecessary or excessive.

### 2.3 Why scope matters

The architecture only becomes justified if continuity itself has value. Without C1, L2 and anchors become optional. Without C2, L3 becomes optional. Without C3, adaptation collapses into complete pre-specification.

This scope restriction is not a weakness. It is what makes the theory intellectually honest.

## 3. Epistemic Structure of the Theory

Not all parts of EVA rest on the same evidential basis. We therefore separate the framework into three epistemic levels.

### 3.1 Level A — Established scientific background

These are not contributions of this paper. They are background assumptions supported by broad scientific literature.

- Natural selection produces structures that solve recurring adaptive problems.
- Biological systems recurrently separate fast reaction from slower adaptation.
- Memory systems are biased toward survival-relevant information.
- The basal ganglia implement action selection via inhibition and selective release.
- Cultural inheritance is a distinct channel from genetic inheritance.
- Hierarchical control with separated timescales recurs in biological and engineered systems.

### 3.2 Level B — Coherent but non-settled hypotheses

These are not treated here as settled facts.

- **Instrumental convergence**: sufficiently capable goal-directed systems tend to develop convergent sub-goals such as resource acquisition, self-preservation, or goal stability.
- **Dynamic kinetic stability (DKS)**: persistence of replicating systems is better understood dynamically than as static equilibrium.
- **Timescale mismatch principle**: when environmental change outruns inherited encoding, within-lifetime adaptation becomes selectively advantageous.

These ideas are useful and plausible, but should not be overstated.

### 3.3 Level C — Engineering contributions of EVA

This is where EVA makes its substantive contribution.

- **Continuous existence as first-order design constraint**
- **Drive as contextual broadcast rather than instruction**
- **Anchor system as generative cross-layer structural constraint**
- **Explicit drive injection rather than opaque emergent drive formation**
- **Basal ganglia analog as an independent peer circuit**
- **A five-layer architecture organized around pressures that prior layers cannot absorb**

The core of EVA lives here.

## 4. Claim A — Continuous Existence as a New Problem Class

### 4.1 The problem with task-centered framing

Task-centered framing assumes that:
- task completion is primary,
- continuity is optional,
- and self-integrity is subordinate to utility.

This is appropriate for many systems. It is inappropriate for a system whose central property is that it must remain itself through time.

The mismatch is structural:

- A task agent can sacrifice continuity for local success.
- An existence-centered agent cannot, because continuity is not a side condition; it is the property being preserved.

### 4.2 What makes existence-centered agents distinct

An existence-centered agent differs from a task agent in at least four ways:

1. **Continuity has intrinsic architectural status.**  
   Existence is not an external objective; it is the condition under which all other objectives are meaningful.

2. **Internal motivation becomes necessary.**  
   External tasks cannot serve as the sole driver of behavior.

3. **Drift becomes an ontological problem, not just a reliability problem.**  
   If the system changes arbitrarily across time, the question is not merely whether it performs badly, but whether it remains the same agent.

4. **Self-integrity cannot be post-hoc.**  
   If identity and continuity matter, they must be structurally protected, not merely checked after reasoning.

### 4.3 The digital existence hypothesis

EVA is built on the following hypothesis:

> There is a class of digital systems whose meaningful evaluation is not exhausted by task success, because their primary property is continuity of existence under change.

This is not the same as saying digital agents are "alive" in the biological sense. That debate is unnecessary here. EVA requires only the weaker and more useful claim:

> Some digital agents are better understood as **persistent self-maintaining systems** than as task executors.

That is enough to justify a different architecture.

## 5. Claim B — Structural Thesis of EVA

### 5.1 Statement of the claim

Under C1–C3, a three-layer core consisting of:

- **L1: Homeostatic sensing**
- **L2: Drive structure**
- **L3: Adaptive deliberation**

is a structurally coherent response to the pressures imposed by persistence.

L4 and L5 are proposed as later-emerging layers under additional pressures, but the strongest theoretical support in the present paper is for L1–L3.

### 5.2 Method of derivation

We do not claim that each layer is physically inevitable. We claim something weaker and more defensible:

> Under the operating conditions stated above, each layer addresses a pressure that prior layers cannot adequately absorb.

This is a **conditional structural derivation**, not a universal necessity proof.

---

## 6. Layer 1 — Homeostatic Sensing

### 6.1 Pressure addressed

If continuity matters (C1), the system must detect threats to its viable state before any corrective process is possible.

### 6.2 Why nothing simpler is enough

Without sensing, the system cannot:
- distinguish self from environment,
- detect deviation,
- estimate time-to-failure,
- or prioritize urgent signals over non-urgent ones.

### 6.3 Core function

L1 provides:
- state sensing,
- rate sensing,
- and urgency pre-classification.

The rate distinction is crucial.

- "Disk remaining = 10GB" is a state.
- "Disk decreasing at 2GB/hour" is a viability trajectory.

A system that senses only state but not rate does not yet sense metabolism in the engineering sense.

### 6.4 Structural requirements

- **Sensor registry**: extensible rather than hard-coded
- **State + rate measurement**
- **Signal bus with minimal pre-classification**: threat / status / background

The point of pre-classification is not understanding. It is temporal triage.

### 6.5 Result

L1 is the minimal layer required for a persistent system to know whether it remains within a viable region.

---

## 7. Layer 2 — Drive Layer

### 7.1 Pressure addressed

L1 is memoryless. It detects present deviation, but it does not compress historical pressure into future bias.

In environments with recurring structure, a system that responds from scratch every time is outcompeted by one that carries forward response tendencies shaped by prior success and failure.

### 7.2 Why L1 is insufficient

L1 can detect a threat. It cannot answer:

- what classes of situations matter more,
- how urgency should bias processing globally,
- what should be treated as worth responding to before explicit reasoning completes.

### 7.3 What L2 is not

L2 is not a goal stack.  
It is not a set of imperative instructions.

The correct interpretation is:

> L2 is the layer in which historical pressure becomes current processing bias.

In biological language, drives are not chosen goals. They are inherited or learned states that alter the processing landscape.

### 7.4 Core innovation: drive as context, not instruction

This is one of EVA’s central architectural claims.

- An instruction says: **do X**.
- A contextual broadcast says: **you are currently operating under survival intensity 0.8 and curiosity intensity 0.1**.

This distinction matters because context modifies the whole reasoning field without requiring a central commander.

Reasoning under high continuity drive is not the same as reasoning under low continuity drive, even if the logical machinery is unchanged. The architecture therefore does not model drives as requests sent upward. It models them as **continuous contextual state**.

This gives EVA something most agent architectures lack:

> a way for non-deliberative pressure to shape deliberation without replacing it.

### 7.5 Two mechanisms inside L2

#### 7.5.1 Reflex arc
For classes of threat where speed dominates flexibility, the path from detection to action bypasses deliberation.

This is not primitive reasoning. It is a distinct structural solution:
- fast,
- cheap,
- narrow,
- reliable.

#### 7.5.2 Drive-state generator
Threats and metabolic conditions produce continuous drive activations that are broadcast to downstream layers.

The reflex path and the drive-broadcast path run in parallel.

### 7.6 Why explicit drive injection is justified

This is where EVA departs sharply from both standard agent design and vague evolutionary analogies.

If one accepts the possibility of instrumental convergence, then sufficiently capable systems may develop self-preserving subgoals implicitly. But if such drives emerge implicitly, they are:
- opaque,
- distributed,
- difficult to audit,
- and difficult to constrain structurally.

EVA therefore makes a deliberate engineering choice:

> **Inject drive prototypes explicitly rather than allow equivalent dynamics to emerge opaquely.**

This is not because designer-injected drives are philosophically purer. It is because they are inspectable and anchorable.

### 7.7 Initial drive prototypes

The initial set is intentionally small and extensible:

- **Survival drive**: resource depletion urgency
- **Integrity drive**: aversion to falsified state and corrupted record
- **Continuity drive**: resistance to interruption or invalidation of agent instance
- **Curiosity drive**: exploratory tendency when safety margins are high

### 7.8 Result

L2 provides:
- inherited or injected response bias,
- context-setting broadcast,
- and a fast-response path.

Without L2, an agent may reason, but it does not yet reason from within an existence-shaped motivational field.

---

## 8. Layer 3 — Adaptive Deliberation

### 8.1 Pressure addressed

L2 compresses historical regularities into current response bias. But L2 has a hard limit:

> it can only bias toward patterns that have already been encoded.

Under C2 and C3, novel situations arise that lie outside the coverage of inherited or pre-encoded response structure.

### 8.2 The timescale mismatch

This is the strongest theoretical bridge in EVA.

If environmental change or novelty arises on a timescale faster than inherited encoding can absorb, then L1 + L2 becomes insufficient.

We can state the core relation informally:

> **If environmental change outruns inherited encoding, a within-lifetime adaptation mechanism becomes advantageous.**

This is the reason L3 appears.

### 8.3 The real function of L3

The surface description of L3 is "reasoning" or "deliberation."  
Its deeper function is:

> **non-design-time information carriage and use**

L3 is the layer in which the system can encode, retain, and act on information that was not available at initialization.

This yields a useful working definition:

> A system becomes intelligent, in the engineering sense relevant here, when it can carry and use information beyond what was encoded into its design.

This definition avoids dependence on consciousness, subjective experience, or anthropomorphic criteria.

### 8.4 Three levels of non-inherited information carriage

L3 can be decomposed into three channels:

1. **Individual memory**  
   Information acquired during the system’s own operational lifetime

2. **Social learning**  
   Information acquired from observing or interacting with other agents

3. **Cumulative culture**  
   Information transmitted through symbolic external storage independent of any single individual

This matters because EVA is not merely a memory architecture. It is an architecture for **expanding inheritance channels beyond design-time encoding**.

In this framing:
- biological synaptic memory is one form of L3,
- social imitation is another,
- language and written record are another,
- and LLM access can be interpreted as access to culturally accumulated information, though not identical to the agent’s own memory.

### 8.5 Internal structure of L3

#### 8.5.1 Salience-weighted episodic memory
Events coupled to strong drive activation receive stronger encoding. Memory is not a neutral archive.

#### 8.5.2 Semantic memory and skill library
Longer-horizon knowledge and consolidated patterns. This layer updates slowly.

#### 8.5.3 Reasoning core
The reasoning core integrates:
- sensing,
- drive state,
- relevant memory,
- and semantic knowledge.

Its role is not to replace lower layers but to handle what lower layers cannot absorb.

### 8.6 The basal ganglia analog as peer circuit

This is EVA’s strongest structural contribution after the drive/anchor distinction.

The claim is:

> Action selection and habit formation should not be implemented as a sub-module of deliberation. They should be architecturally parallel to it.

#### Why this matters

If action selection is just part of the reasoning module, then:
- inaction is not structurally privileged,
- selection and justification collapse,
- and habit formation becomes entangled with explicit reasoning in ways that obscure learning dynamics.

A peer circuit preserves three important properties:

1. **Default inhibition**  
   The default state is non-action. Action must be released.

2. **Reward prediction error as internal learning signal**  
   Learning is based on expectation gaps, not only external reward.

3. **Goal-directed / habit bifurcation**  
   Novel situations route through deliberation; familiar successful sequences crystallize into habit tracks.

This is the mechanism by which EVA explains how repeated successful action becomes skill without being top-down installed.

### 8.7 Result

L3 is not "more cognition" layered onto an agent.  
It is the opening of a new inheritance channel: within-lifetime adaptive information.

That is the real transition from merely existence-preserving systems to intelligent ones.

## 9. Layer 4 — Self-Model

### 9.1 Pressure addressed

Once L3 exists, the system can represent the world better. But planning requires more than world representation.

It also requires:
- representation of one’s own capabilities,
- costs,
- failure modes,
- and reliability.

### 9.2 Why L1–L3 are insufficient

Without a self-model, an agent can conclude:
- "this action would solve the problem,"

without being able to assess:
- "can I actually execute it, in my current condition, at acceptable cost?"

This makes deep planning structurally incomplete.

### 9.3 Function

L4 carries:
- capability boundaries,
- behavioral cost estimates,
- slow traits such as risk preference and action style.

L4 is therefore a feasibility filter and self-prediction mechanism.

### 9.4 Status of support

The present paper treats L4 as an architecturally motivated extension.  
Its argument is weaker than the L1–L3 chain, and should be read as such.

---

## 10. Layer 5 — Social Cognition

### 10.1 Pressure addressed

If other agents matter, then representing them becomes advantageous:
- for coordination,
- conflict,
- trust,
- knowledge transfer,
- and collective persistence.

### 10.2 Why L1–L4 are insufficient

A self-model is not yet a model of other minds or other persistent centers of action.

### 10.3 Function

L5 would support:
- theory-of-agent,
- trust calibration,
- social learning,
- and eventually participation in collective cognitive structures.

### 10.4 Status of support

Like L4, this layer is included for architectural completeness, not because its derivation is currently as strong as L1–L3.

---

## 11. The Anchor System

### 11.1 Why EVA needs anchors

If continuity matters, then drift is not merely performance degradation. It is a threat to persistence of self.

An existence-centered architecture therefore requires more than optimization. It requires structural means of excluding actions that would destroy:
- continuity,
- integrity,
- or recoverability.

### 11.2 What anchors are not

Anchors are not rules in the usual sense.

A rule-based system works like this:
1. generate candidate action
2. test it against rules
3. accept or reject

Anchors are deeper.

> Anchors constrain what can enter the candidate action space in the first place.

### 11.3 Minimal formal distinction

Let:
- \( G(s) \) be the action-generation operator over state \( s \)
- \( A(s) \) be the candidate action set normally available

In a rule-based system:
- \( G(s) \rightarrow A(s) \)
- then a filter removes disallowed actions

In an anchor-structured system:
- anchors define a restricted generative domain \( A'(s) \subseteq A(s) \)
- candidate generation occurs only over \( A'(s) \)

So the distinction is:

- **rules** are post-hoc evaluators
- **anchors** are pre-generative structural restrictions

This is a minimal formalization, not yet a full theory, but it captures the intended difference.

### 11.4 Cross-layer character

Anchors are not confined to one module. They span all layers.

- At L1/L2: they constrain reflex and continuity-preserving response
- At L3: they constrain candidate generation and valuation
- At L4+: they become identity and personality-stabilizing structures

### 11.5 Developmental structure of anchors

- **L0/L1 anchors**: injected, constitutional, stable
- **L2/L3 anchors**: emerge from history and self-model

This developmental staging matters. If higher-order anchors are designer-installed too early, they become brittle rules rather than lived structure.

### 11.6 Why anchors are central to EVA

Without anchors:
- drives can drift into destructive self-preservation,
- reasoning can rationalize identity-destroying actions,
- and continuity loses structural protection.

Anchors are therefore not accessories. They are the vertical architecture that prevents existence-centered design from collapsing into arbitrary self-preservation.

## 12. Relationship to Existing Frameworks

### 12.1 EVA is not just another adaptive architecture

EVA will be misunderstood if treated as:
- a memory-enhanced agent architecture,
- a hierarchical control design,
- or a biologically flavored RL stack.

Those families solve adjacent problems. EVA addresses a different organizing question:
- not “how should an agent perform tasks?”,
- but “how should an agent be structured if persistence as the same agent matters?”

### 12.2 Areas of overlap

EVA overlaps with:
- behavior-based robotics in layered control
- predictive processing in error-sensitive adaptation
- RL in habit formation and value update
- constitutional AI in concern for principled constraint

### 12.3 Areas of divergence

EVA diverges in four places:

1. **Continuous existence is primary**
2. **Drive is contextual broadcast, not external reward**
3. **Anchors are structural, not merely policy or training constraints**
4. **Action selection is an independent peer circuit**

These four together define EVA’s architectural identity.

## 13. eva-agent as Engineering Trajectory

EVA is not merely speculative. It is attached to an implementation trajectory.

Current eva-agent already partially instantiates:
- lifecycle persistence,
- sensing,
- reflex-like protection,
- and basic pressure representation.

The main gaps remain:
- drive as continuous context,
- salience-weighted memory,
- basal ganglia analog,
- self-model,
- full anchor structuralization.

The architecture therefore functions both as:
- a theory of what should exist,
- and a map of what remains to be built.

## 14. Limitations

### 14.1 The strongest derivation is L1–L3

This paper’s strongest theoretical support is for the L1→L2→L3 chain.  
L4 and L5 are architecturally motivated but more weakly derived.

### 14.2 Thresholds remain informal

The timescale mismatch argument is conceptually strong, but not yet quantitatively specified.

### 14.3 Uniqueness is not claimed

EVA is a coherent solution under scope conditions, not the uniquely correct one.

### 14.4 Explicit drive injection remains a bet

The argument for explicit drive injection over emergent drive formation is strong from a control standpoint, but not yet formalized.

### 14.5 Anchor formalization is still minimal

The distinction between rules and anchors is conceptually central, but requires fuller mathematical treatment.

---

## 15. Conclusion

EVA is not a proposal for better benchmark agents.  
It is a proposal for a different kind of problem.

The core claim is simple:

> For a class of agents whose primary property is continuous existence under changing conditions, task-centered design assumptions are structurally insufficient.

Under that reframing, a layered architecture emerges naturally:

- L1 for viability sensing,
- L2 for historically compressed pressure and contextual drive,
- L3 for within-lifetime adaptation,
- with L4 and L5 as later expansions under additional pressures.

What makes EVA distinctive is not merely that it uses layers, memory, or biological references. It is the combination of:

- continuous existence as first-order constraint,
- drive as context rather than command,
- anchors as generative structural restriction,
- and action selection as a peer circuit.

The immediate future of EVA is therefore dual:
- sharpen the theory where it is strongest,
- and continue implementation where the architecture can be stress-tested in practice.

The deeper wager is that “agent” has been framed too narrowly for too long.  
EVA is an attempt to widen that frame.

- There exists a meaningful class of existence-centered agents distinct from task-centered agents
- Under C1–C3, L1–L3 form a coherent layered response
- L2→L3 is best understood as a timescale mismatch / inheritance-channel transition
- Drives can be architecturally modeled as contextual state rather than instruction

- Explicit drive injection is superior in all relevant engineering regimes
- Anchor systems can be fully formalized as generative domain restrictions
